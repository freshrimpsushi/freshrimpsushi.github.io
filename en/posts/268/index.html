Overview

The Hindmarsh-Rose model is a model that explains the changes in voltage related to ion channels in neurons. The term memristor is a concept combining memory and resistor. This document describes a system that adds a memristor to the Hindmarsh-Rose model.

Model 1

The dimensionless system of the memristor-based Hindmarsh-Rose circuit and the equivalent differential equations can be represented as the following non-autonomous non-smooth system. $$ \begin{align*} \dot{x} =& y - ax^{3} + bx^{2} + kxz + I \\ \dot{y} =& c - dx^{2} - y \\ \dot{z} =& \alpha g (z) + \beta x \end{align*} $$

Here, $I = f \cos \left( \omega t \right)$ denotes the external current, and $g(z)$ is a function defined as follows: $$ \begin{align*} g(z) =& \sign \left( z + 1 \right) + \sign \left( z - 1 \right) - z \\ =& \begin{cases} -2 -z & , \text{if } z < -1 \\ -z & , \text{if } -1 \le z \le 1 \\ 2 - z & , \text{if } z > 1 \end{cases} \end{align*} $$

Variables2

  • $x(t)$: This is the variable associated with membrane potential at the point $t$.
  • $y(t)$: This is the spiking variable related to the movement of sodium and potassium ion channels at the point $t$.
  • $z(t)$: This is the variable related to the adaptation current at the point $t$.

Parameters

  • $a=1$, $b=3$, $c=1$, $d=5$: These values are related to resistance.
  • $\omega = 1$: This is the period of the external current.
  • $f = 0.1$: This is the magnitude of the external current.
  • $k = 0.9$: This denotes the coupling strength.
  • $\alpha = 0.1$, $\beta = 0.8$: These are memristor synapse parameters.

Explanation

The memristor Hindmarsh-Rose neuron model, hereinafter referred to as the HR model, is characterized by having three subsystems, dividing the domain into two planes $z = \pm 1$ as a non-smooth system.

Bifurcation

If one sets $f$ as the bifurcation parameter and plots a bifurcation diagram, as shown above, it demonstrates characteristic behaviors at values below $f^{\ast} \approx 0.21$.

function RK4(f::Function, v::AbstractVector, h=1e-2, nonsmooth=0.0)
    V1 = f(v, nonsmooth)
    V2 = f(v + (h/2)*V1, nonsmooth)
    V3 = f(v + (h/2)*V2, nonsmooth)
    V4 = f(v + h*V3, nonsmooth)
    return v + (h/6)*(V1 + 2V2 + 2V3 + V4), V1
end
